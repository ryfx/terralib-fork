Building Dispersion Graphics

Another type of useful graphic is the Dispersion graphic, which can be used to analyze the behavior of two variables at once. For instance, the analysis of mean monthly income in relation to the size of illiterate population in Belo Horizonte census tracts could indicate if some sector has a different behavior. Reactivate the Views tree display and activate SetoresCensitarios theme. Use the popup menu to select Graphic Parameters to open the creation of graphics interface. Choose Graphic Type as Dispersion, select in Attribute(X) the sectors mean income data (REN_MED), and in Attribute(Y) the sector illiterate percentage (P_ANALF):

The created graphic has in X axis the mean monthly income and in Y axis the illiterate percentage. Each point of the graphic represents a census tract: its mean income is presented by the value in X axis and the illiteracy rate is presented by the value in Y axis. Observe that BH census tracts follow a strong inverse proportionality behavior between mean monthly income and illiteracy rate: the higher a sector monthly income is, the smaller its illiterate population is. Notice that the points create a curve that nears both axes.

Sectors with high illiteracy rates have very low monthly income and there are sectors with mean monthly income above R$ 10.000,00. To verify on the map where the areas with high illiteracy rate are, use the brush tool. Activate the Draw area. Then select Graphic Cursor and, in the Graphic area, change its size (using Alt and Crtl keys in a similar way to selection of map objects) to select the five points with the highest illiterate percentage. The map will not appear different since the selected census tracts are very small. Use the reduction factor at the window upper right corner to select 25000. One of the selected sectors will appear in green; to visualize the others, use Pan Cursor and drag the cursor on the map.

The sectors, in addition to being very small, are not close to each other, indicating that the high illiteracy rate is not due to their spatial relation. Dispersion graphics can be used and are very efficient to analyze variables that evolve with time, such as the number of homicides per year for a period of years. Regions where the number has increased or decreased differently to the expected behavior can be found.
